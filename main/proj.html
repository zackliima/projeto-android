History of The Android Mascot

You probably know that the Android 🔗 operating system, maintained by Google 🔗, is one of the most widely used mobile devices worldwide. But perhaps you didn't know that your friendly mascot has a name and a very interesting story? Well, follow this article to learn a lot about this little robot.

The First Version

The first attempt at creating a mascot came in 2007 from a developer named Dan Morrill 🔗. He says he opened Inkscape 🔗 (free software for vectorizing images) and created his own version of the robot. The goal was to personify the system for his team; there was no request from the company to create a mascot.

This first bizarre version was even named after its creator: they would be the Dandroids.

A New Mascot Emerges

The idea of ​​having a mascot matured, and the task was passed on to a professional in the field. Russian illustrator Irina Blok 🔗, also a Google employee, was tasked with representing the little robot in a more appealing way.

Irina's main idea was to represent everything graphically with fewer lines and in a flatter way. The drawing should also be easily identifiable to anyone looking at it. This, Bugdroid, Android's new mascot, was born.

The main inspiration for the new Bugdroid's design came from those little figures used to illustrate bathroom doors, indicating the type of door. Legend has it that the artist was creating at her desk in the Google office and looked at the bathroom side, and the identification was immediate: simple, clean, and objective.

Want to learn More?

Another curious fact about Android is that each version has always been named after a sweet treat, in alphabetical order from version 1.5 to 9.0.

  • 1.5 - Cupcake
  • 1.6 - Donut
  • 3.0 - Eclair
  • 2.2 - Froyo
  • 2.3 - Gingerbread
  • 3.0 - Honeycomb
  • 4.0 - Ice Cream Sandwich
  • 4.1 - Jelly Bean
  • 4.4 - KitKat
  • 5.0 - Lolipop
  • 6.0 - Marshmallow
  • 7.0 - Nougat
  • 8.0 - Oreo
  • 9.0 - Pie

Unfortunately, Android Q never happened, as Google decided to end this fun practice and started using numbering, which gave rise to Android 10.
